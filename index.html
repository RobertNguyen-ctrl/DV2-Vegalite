Where & What — Location and Sport Choices

Participation rates by state, and Australia's most popular activities.

ACT leads the nation — Walking dominates everywhere

The ACT (72.1%) is Australia's most active territory while the NT (54.7%) sits lowest — a 17-point gap driven by infrastructure and demographics.

Nationally, Walking (6.8M participants) dwarfs all other activities. The top 5 are all individual and non-competitive — no team, no club required.

Organised sports like AFL and Cricket appear much further down, reflecting a national shift toward flexible, informal activity.

Key insight: Where you live shapes how active you are. Warmer states + accessible activities = higher participation.

Age & gender shape sport choices dramatically

Gym & fitness peaks at 15–24 (48%) then declines sharply with age. Walking does the opposite — growing to 74% at 65+.

AFL and Netball are youth sports — participation collapses after 35. Golf is the rare exception that gets more popular with age.

On gender: Rugby League (+38%) and Cricket (+34%) skew heavily male. Netball (−28%) and Dance (−24%) lean female. Walking and Swimming are near-perfectly balanced.

Key insight: Team sports show the largest gender gaps. Individual activities are far more balanced across both age and gender.

Trends & Behaviour — How participation is changing

Long-term growth, COVID impact, and the shift from organised to informal sport.

Australia is more active than ever — but prefers to go it alone

Participation hit a record 64.3% in 2024 after a COVID crash to 59.2% in 2020. The bounce-back surpassed pre-COVID levels by 2022.

Walking (94% informal), Running (81%), and Cycling (79%) are almost entirely unorganised. Only Cricket (78%), Netball (74%), and AFL (71%) remain club-dependent.

Key insight: The future of Australian sport is informal and individual — sporting bodies must rethink engagement models to reach the unaffiliated majority.

Why & Why Not — Motivations and Barriers

What drives Australians to be active — and what holds them back?

Health drives participation — time and cost block it

Physical health (84%) and mental wellbeing (71%) are the top reasons Australians exercise. Enjoyment and social connection also rank highly, suggesting sport serves both body and mind.

On the barrier side, "Not enough time" (43%) is the dominant obstacle — cited across all age groups. Cost (31%) and injury/health concerns (28%) follow.

Notably, "Not interested" (18%) is relatively low — most inactive Australians want to be active but face practical barriers rather than motivational ones.

Key insight: Policy should focus on reducing time and cost barriers — the motivation to participate is already there for most Australians.

Padel & Pickleball surge — traditional sports struggle

Padel (+41%) and Pickleball (+38%) are the fastest growing sports in Australia, driven by social media exposure and low equipment barriers. Yoga/Pilates (+18%) continues its steady climb.

Meanwhile, Cricket (−8%) and Lawn Bowls (−11%) are declining — reflecting an aging participant base that isn't being replaced by younger generations.

Across age groups, 65+ participation has grown the most since 2016 — up 8 points — as older Australians embrace active ageing. Youth participation (15–24) has remained flat.

Key insight: The sports landscape is reshaping fast. Emerging racket sports and mind-body activities are capturing the market that traditional team sports are losing.
