Where & What — Location and Sport Choices

Participation rates by state, and Australia's most popular activities.

ACT leads the nation — Walking dominates everywhere

The ACT (72.1%) is Australia's most active territory, nearly 18 points above the NT (54.7%). This gap reflects deep structural differences: ACT has Australia's highest median income, most educated workforce, and best access to green spaces and cycling infrastructure.

Nationally, Walking (6.8M) has nearly double the participants of Swimming (3.9M) — the #2 sport. The top 5 activities are all individual and require no registration, no fixed schedule, and minimal cost.

Organised team sports like AFL, Netball, and Cricket don't appear until positions 10–13 — a dramatic shift from how Australians thought about sport just 20 years ago.

Key insight: Geography and socioeconomics are powerful predictors of activity. States with warmer climates, higher incomes, and better infrastructure consistently outperform the national average.

Age & gender shape sport choices more than any other factor

The heatmap shows Gym & Fitness dominates at 15–24 (48%) but collapses to just 12% at 65+. This isn't just aging — it reflects a generational identity shift: young Australians treat fitness as lifestyle, older ones treat walking as medicine.

Golf is the only sport that grows continuously with age, peaking at 65+ — making it uniquely positioned as Australia's "retirement sport." Meanwhile AFL and Netball effectively cease to exist past age 44.

The gender diverging chart reveals that the most gender-skewed sports are all team sports. The least skewed are all individual activities — suggesting social and cultural norms around team sport are far more gendered than physical activity itself.

Key insight: Australia needs age-specific sport pathways. Retaining gym-goers past 35 and converting AFL/Netball players into lifelong active participants are two of the biggest untapped opportunities in sport policy.

Trends & Behaviour — How participation is changing

Long-term growth, COVID impact, and the shift from organised to informal sport.

COVID reshaped Australia's activity habits — permanently

The 2020 crash to 59.2% was the sharpest single-year decline on record. But the recovery wasn't simply a return to normal — it was a restructuring. Activities that could be done alone outdoors (walking, cycling, running) surged and never came back down.

By 2024, Australia hit a record 64.3% — but this growth is concentrated in informal activity. The stacked bar shows Walking (94% informal) and Running (81%) are essentially ungoverned activities with no club, no fixture, no membership fee.

This creates a structural crisis for sporting bodies: participation is up, but affiliated membership — and therefore revenue and influence — is stagnating or declining for most traditional sports.

Key insight: COVID didn't kill Australian sport — it accelerated a trend already underway. The future belongs to flexible, low-cost, individual activities. Clubs that don't adapt risk irrelevance.

Why & Why Not — Motivations and Barriers

What drives Australians to be active — and what holds them back?

Health drives participation — time and cost block it

Physical health (84%) and mental wellbeing (71%) are the top reasons Australians exercise. Enjoyment and social connection also rank highly, suggesting sport serves both body and mind.

On the barrier side, "Not enough time" (43%) is the dominant obstacle — cited across all age groups. Cost (31%) and injury/health concerns (28%) follow.

Notably, "Not interested" (18%) is relatively low — most inactive Australians want to be active but face practical barriers rather than motivational ones.

Key insight: Policy should focus on reducing time and cost barriers — the motivation to participate is already there for most Australians.

Padel & Pickleball are rewriting the sports landscape

Padel (+41%) and Pickleball (+38%) are growing faster than any traditional sport in Australian history. Both are social, low-impact, easy to learn, and playable in smaller spaces — ticking every box that modern participants demand.

The decline of Cricket (−8%) and Lawn Bowls (−11%) reflects an aging, non-renewing participant base. Both sports have failed to attract under-35s at scale, and their summer/weekly scheduling conflicts with increasingly fragmented leisure time.

The age-group trend chart shows the most important long-term story: 65+ participation has grown by 8 percentage points since 2016 — the largest gain of any group. Australia's aging population is becoming more active, not less. Youth (15–24) remains flat, suggesting significant untapped potential.

Key insight: The next decade of Australian sport will be defined by two groups: emerging social racket sports capturing 25–45 year olds, and active-ageing programs for the 65+ boom. Traditional sports associations that fail to evolve risk irrelevance within a generation.
