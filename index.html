Where are Australians most active?

Participation rates vary significantly across states and territories.

The ACT leads the nation — the NT lags behind

The Australian Capital Territory records the highest participation rate at 72.1% — driven by its younger, highly educated, and public-sector-employed population with easy access to parks and facilities.

The Northern Territory sits at the other end at 54.7%, reflecting geographic isolation, infrastructure gaps, and a different demographic profile.

Western Australia (67.9%) and Queensland (66.8%) benefit from warmer climates and outdoor culture, while Tasmania (58.3%) faces challenges of remoteness.

Key insight: A 17.4 percentage point gap exists between the most and least active state — suggesting that where you live significantly shapes how active you are.

Walking is the undisputed #1 — by a huge margin

With over 6.8 million participants, walking dwarfs every other activity. It requires no equipment, no club membership, and no specific skill — making it universally accessible.

The top 5 are dominated by individual, non-competitive activities: walking, swimming, gym/fitness, cycling, and running. Organised team sports like AFL, netball, and cricket appear much further down.

This reflects a broader national shift toward informal, flexible physical activity over structured sport — a trend accelerated post-COVID.

Key insight: The top 5 activities account for nearly 60% of all participations — and none of them require a team or a competition.

Who plays what — and at what age?

Different sports attract vastly different age profiles.

Age shapes sport choices dramatically

The heatmap reveals clear generational patterns. Gym & fitness peaks strongly among 15–24 year-olds (48%) and declines sharply with age. Walking shows the opposite — participation grows steadily, peaking at 74% for those 65+.

AFL and Netball are largely youth sports — participation collapses after age 35. Meanwhile Golf is the rare sport that grows more popular with age, peaking in the 65+ group.

Running/Jogging shows a sharp inverted-V, peaking in the 25–34 bracket and declining quickly — reflecting the physical toll of high-impact exercise over time.

Key insight: Designing sport programs that retain participants as they age requires fundamentally different activities for different life stages.
